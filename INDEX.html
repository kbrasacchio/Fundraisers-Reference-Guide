Welcome & Orientation

This reference guide is your companion to NXT training, more detailed than a workbook, less intimidating than a manual. It’s designed for frontline fundraisers who manage prospect relationships, track gift opportunities, analyze giving trends, and use data to drive strategy. You don’t need to become a database expert, but you do need to know the system well enough to do your job with confidence.

Raiser’s Edge NXT is Blackbaud’s current generation of the fundraising database St. Andrew’s has used for years. Unlike its predecessor, NXT runs entirely in a web browser—accessible from any device, no software to install. At its core, NXT is the same database as before; what’s changed is the interface and the way you interact with it day to day.

How to use this guide Jump to any section using the table of contents. Interactive diagrams and definition cards open pop-up panels when clicked. If you’re reading this before training, focus on Sections 2–6 first. Section 11 has a full RE7 → NXT term translation table for quick reference.

As you work through this guide, you’ll notice that NXT uses different terminology than the older Database View interface—and that some of those terms are still in flux as Blackbaud completes its transition. Section 2 addresses this directly. A note on your own title: in the database view you’re called a Solicitor; in the web view you’re a Fundraiser. Same role, different label. This guide uses web view terminology throughout.

Terminology note It’s technically incorrect—though extremely common—to refer to the web view as “NXT.” The full product is Raiser’s Edge NXT, which contains both views. This matters when talking with your DBA or reading Blackbaud documentation.
Coming from RE7? The database view is the same software as The Raiser’s Edge version 7. If colleagues say “RE7,” “the old view,” or “the desktop version,” they mean the database view.
Database View retirement—first half of 2027 Blackbaud is retiring the database view in the first half of 2027 to what Blackbaud is calling the “Total Web Solution” or Unified View. Your organization is actively transitioning all workflows ahead of this deadline.

The Workcenter

The Workcenter is your fundraising command center—a single dashboard that surfaces your portfolio, pending actions, recent gifts, open opportunities, and unassigned prospects. Checking it at the start of each day keeps your pipeline moving and ensures nothing slips through the cracks.

Suggested morning routine Make the Workcenter your first stop after email. Actions anything past due or due today Gifts recent giving that warrants a same-day thank-you Portfolio anyone whose stage needs updating Opportunities open asks that need movement. 10–15 minutes keeps your pipeline current and ensures nothing gets missed.

Portfolio

Your Portfolio is your assigned list of prospects—the relationships you are accountable for cultivating and moving toward a gift. The Portfolio view shows each prospect’s current stage, last action date, assigned fundraisers, and giving summary—everything you need to assess the health of a relationship at a glance without opening each record.

Prospect stages track where a constituent is in your cultivation process. Common stages include: Identification, Qualification, Cultivation, Solicitation, and Stewardship. Keeping stages current is one of the most valuable data practices you can maintain; an outdated stage gives leadership a false picture of the pipeline.

Prospect Manager vs. Frontline Fundraiser Some fundraisers carry both roles simultaneously.
A Prospect Manager has strategic oversight of a group and sees past-due actions from all fundraisers working assigned prospects.
A Frontline Fundraiser makes the calls and direct asks. Both roles appear in the Workcenter at the same time.
Check your portfolio regularly Don’t wait until a meeting or a reporting deadline to review your portfolio. A quick weekly scan catches stalled stages, overdue actions, and prospects who need re-engagement before they slip too far.

Prospect stages—what they mean

StageWhat it means
IdentificationThe prospect has been identified as a potential donor or major gift candidate but has not yet been formally qualified.
QualificationResearch and initial contact are underway to determine the prospect’s capacity, interests, and readiness to give.
CultivationAn active relationship-building phase—deepening engagement, learning priorities, building trust before an ask.
SolicitationAn ask has been made and a response is pending. The Opportunity is active and expected to close.
StewardshipA gift has been received and the relationship is being nurtured for the next cycle of engagement and giving.
ClosedThe Opportunity has concluded—either a gift was received or the ask was declined. Stage should be updated promptly.
Stale stages are a pipeline liability A prospect stuck in “Cultivation” for 18 months with no recent actions tells leadership nothing useful and suggests the relationship may have been neglected. Review stages weekly and update immediately when something changes.
2
Gifts

The Gifts section surfaces recent gift activity tied to your portfolio—new gifts, pending pledge payments, and anything needing attention—without having to open each individual record. Filter by date range, campaign, or fund.

Set up a persistent Gifts List filtered to your portfolio as a live replacement for emailed daily gift reports—the list stays current, never stale, and never travels unsecured through email.

Every gift is a same-day follow-up trigger When a gift comes in from one of your prospects, log a thank-you Action the same day. It takes two minutes and is the single highest-return activity in relationship fundraising. A timely acknowledgment builds the history that matters when it’s time for the next ask.
Gifts as intelligence A gift to a fund you didn’t expect, a first-ever gift from a lapsed donor, or an unusually large annual gift are all signals. Make it a habit to review the Gifts section with an eye for what’s surprising—not just what’s expected.
3
Actions

The Actions section is your personal to-do list—upcoming and overdue interactions across all your assigned prospects. It shows actions assigned directly to you and, importantly, past-due actions from other fundraisers linked to your prospects if you are their Prospect Manager.

Past-due actions are visible to your manager When your actions go past due, they appear in your Prospect Manager’s Workcenter. This is intentional design—keeping actions current and marked Completed is part of how your activity is visible to leadership and how you maintain credibility across the team.
Past-due from other team members Even if an action was not assigned to you, it will appear in your Workcenter if it’s overdue and linked to one of your portfolio prospects. As Prospect Manager, you are responsible for knowing when something is slipping on a relationship you oversee. A past-due action on your prospect is your signal to follow up internally or escalate.

See Section 6 for the full deep-dive on Actions—types, statuses, best practices, and step-by-step logging.

Opportunities

Opportunities (called Proposals in the database view / RE7) represent active asks in your pipeline—each with a prospect, ask amount, stage, expected close date, and likelihood percentage. This is where your major gift pipeline lives.

Opportunity stages

StageWhat it means
IdentificationProspect identified as a potential major gift candidate; research and initial engagement underway.
CultivationActively building the relationship—deepening trust, understanding priorities, preparing for an ask.
SolicitationThe ask has been made and a response is pending.
StewardshipA gift was received; the relationship is being nurtured toward the next cycle.
ClosedThe Opportunity concluded—gift received or ask declined. Update immediately.
Keep stages and close dates current Your pipeline is only as useful as the data in it. An Opportunity in “Solicitation” with a close date six months in the past tells nobody anything and makes your pipeline report unreliable. Review and update weekly.
Likelihood percentage The likelihood field is your estimate of the probability that this ask will close successfully. It’s used by leadership for pipeline forecasting. Use it as a genuine forecasting tool; a 90% likelihood on every open ask tells leadership nothing and makes the pipeline report meaningless.
Stale opportunities undermine the pipeline An Opportunity that hasn’t been updated in months is as misleading as a stale prospect stage. If an ask has been declined, mark it Closed. If a close date has passed, update it. Leadership uses this data for forecasting—outdated opportunities create a false picture of what’s actually in the pipeline.

Unassigned

The Unassigned section shows active constituents in the organization’s prospect pool who have not yet been assigned to a fundraiser. As Prospect Manager, this is your review queue—a list of relationships that may be ready to be claimed, but haven’t been yet.

Reviewing unassigned constituents is part of your portfolio management responsibilities. Scan this list periodically and flag any prospects you recognize as strong candidates for your portfolio or a colleague’s.

Scan for familiar names You may recognize alumni or donors from your network who clearly belong in a portfolio but haven’t been claimed. If you identify a prospect who you believe belongs in your portfolio, flag them for discussion with the larger team.

Gift Management

Understanding how gifts are structured in NXT is foundational for fundraisers —it affects how you read a donor’s record, how you interpret dashboard metrics, and how you discuss giving history with prospects. You don’t need to enter gifts yourself (that’s typically your database administrator’s role), but you do need to understand what you’re looking at.

Fundraisers are read-only for gifts Gift entry, editing, and deletion are handled exclusively by your database administrator. Your role is to read and interpret gift data—not to enter it. If you spot an error on a constituent’s record (wrong fund, missing appeal, incorrect amount), flag it to your DBA rather than attempting to correct it yourself.

Gift types

One-Time Gift
Any single monetary contribution—cash, check, or credit card. The most common gift type.
Stock / Property
Non-cash assets donated to benefit the organization—real estate, common stock, or securities. Sold by the organization upon receipt.
Gift-in-Kind
Donations of goods or services rather than money—equipment, supplies, professional services, or event sponsorship.
Other
Additional donations that don’t fall into the above categories. Usage varies by organization.

Commitments: pledges, recurring, and planned gifts

Pledge
A promise to donate a specific total amount, paid over time in installments. Appears in both Committed and Received revenue as payments come in.
Recurring Gift
A promise to donate the same amount on a regular schedule—monthly, quarterly, annually. Automated once set up.
Planned Gift
A promise to contribute at a future date, often through estate planning—bequests, charitable remainder trusts, or life insurance policies.

Soft credits & matching gifts

Soft Credit
A copy of the gift applied to another constituent’s record—spouse, foundation, employer—without duplicating the actual revenue.
Click for details ›
Matching Gift
A gift matched by an employer or foundation. Tracked separately but linked to the original donor’s record.
Click for details ›

Actions

An Action is the logged record of a personal, one-on-one interaction between your organization and a constituent—a phone call, a meeting, a personal email, a handwritten note. Actions are the primary evidence that relationship work is happening, and they are how the organization maintains institutional memory across staff transitions.

What an action contains

FieldWhat to enter
CategoryCall, Email, Letter/Mail, Meeting, Task
SummaryA short title—“Phone call re: annual fund” or “Thank-you note after pledge”
DateWhen the interaction occurred or is planned
StatusCompleted, Pending, or custom status
Assigned toThe fundraiser responsible (formerly “Solicitor” in RE7)
Notes / CommentsWhat was discussed, agreed, or needs follow-up
Follow-up actionOptional—creates a linked next step from this action

Action categories

Call
Phone or video call with the constituent.
Click for details ›
Email
A personal email, not a mass mailing or newsletter.
Click for details ›
Meeting
In-person or virtual: coffee, cultivation dinner, campus visit, event follow-up.
Click for details ›
Letter / Mail
A personal letter, card, or handwritten note.
Click for details ›
Task
An internal to-do: research, preparation, internal coordination.
Click for details ›
Log same-day. Every time. A two-sentence entry logged immediately after a call is worth more than a perfect entry written a week later. The notes field is where the relationship lives—a call logged with no notes tells future-you and your colleagues almost nothing.
Use follow-up actions to keep momentum If a meeting leads to a promised next step, create a follow-up action before closing the current one. A cultivation dinner that ends with “let’s connect in six weeks” should immediately become a pending Action with a date—not a note in your personal calendar.
Actions vs. Notes—what goes where An Action = a direct interaction with the constituent (call, meeting, email).
A Note = background information or context that doesn’t represent an interaction (company restructuring, personal situation, preferred communication style). Both are essential; they serve different purposes.

Events

Events in NXT track attendance, RSVPs, registration fees, and participation levels for any gathering: cultivation dinners, receptions, galas, phonathons, campus visits. As a fundraiser, events are a cultivation and stewardship tool, and how a donor engages with events is meaningful intelligence for your portfolio strategy.

Event registration in the web view

RSVPs and attendance can be recorded directly on the event record. As guests are confirmed, add them as participants and update their RSVP status in real time. This keeps your attendance data current and automatically updates each constituent’s record.

Web View TermWhat it means
Invitation StatusWhether the person has been formally invited. Was “Invite?” in the database view.
RSVP StatusTheir response-Attending, Declined, Waitlisted, Interested, or No Response.
Participation LevelTheir role at the event-speaker, attendee, sponsor, volunteer, etc.
Registration FeesAny fees associated with attending. Was “Prices or Units” in the database view.
Events as cultivation intelligence A donor who has attended three cultivation dinners but hasn’t made a major gift may be ready for a direct ask. Check the Events tile on a constituent record before any cultivation meeting—attendance history is one of the clearest signals of engagement depth.

Events can also be linked to Actions on a constituent record; logging that you personally invited a prospect to a reception, or following up after they attended. This is how the organization builds a picture of engagement over time, beyond just who showed up.

Event attendance is visible in the Giving tile too For events where a ticket price or registration fee is involved, that payment appears as a gift in the constituent’s giving history. Be aware of this when reading giving totals—event fees can inflate a donor’s apparent giving if not filtered appropriately.

Lists & Queries

NXT gives you two tools for grouping records: Lists and Queries, both now available directly in the web view. Lists are straightforward filters ideal for outreach and email. Queries are more powerful, supporting complex logic and precise data exports. Understanding when to use each is one of the highest-leverage skills you can develop in the system.

Lists vs. Queries at a glance

ListsQueries
Built in the web view, you build it yourselfBuilt in the web view—available to fundraisers with appropriate access
Create groups of constituents, gifts, actions, and opportunitiesCreate groups of any record type—including campaign, fund, appeal—plus all List types
Straightforward filters, AND logic onlyComplex multi-level filters—uses both AND and OR operators
Works directly in web view tools: email, workflows, gift receiptingCan be used directly in standard reports and web view tools when saved as Static
Not recommended for mailings or data exportsCorrect source for mailings and precision exports

AND vs. OR operators

Queries use logical operators to combine filter criteria. The difference between AND and OR changes your results dramatically.

AND
The constituent must meet all criteria to be included. Only the overlap between the two groups is returned.
Example: Class of 2005 AND gave last year—only people who satisfy both conditions.
OR
The constituent must meet at least one criterion to be included. Both groups are returned together.
Example: Class of 2005 OR Class of 2010—everyone from either year is included.
Lists only use ANDLists always apply AND logic between filters. If you need OR logic—or more complex combinations—you need a Query.

Query criteria: Field, Operator, Value

Every query filter is built from three components. Understanding this structure helps you read existing queries and build new ones accurately.

The data point you want to filter on

e.g. Is greater than

One-to-one vs. one-to-many fields

One-to-One Fields
Linked to one record, yield one result. Example: Constituent Name.

Output tab controls what you see in ResultsThe fields you include in the Output tab directly determine what columns appear in the Results tab. If a field is not in Output it will not appear in Results even if you filtered on it. A record may appear multiple times in results if it has multiple values for a one-to-many output field.

Sharing via link—new in NXT

Copy link to query or to results NXT now lets you copy a direct link to a query or to a query’s current results and share it with any colleague who has access. This is a much better alternative to exporting a spreadsheet and emailing it; the link is always live, the data is always current, and nothing travels through email as an attachment.
Export protocols—use with intention Exporting data creates a snapshot that goes stale immediately. Exported files travel through email, sit on desktops, and create security risks. Before exporting: (1) confirm the export is actually necessary; (2) use Query output—not List output—to ensure clean, unduplicated data; (3) delete the file when you’re done with it. When possible, share a live link instead.

Dashboards

Dashboards in NXT give you a visual overview of your fundraising data—giving trends, pipeline health, donor lifecycle status, and more. There are two types: Standard (pre-configured dashboards built by Blackbaud) and Custom (dashboards you build yourself to focus on the metrics your organization cares about most).

Revenue types—critical to understand

NXT dashboards display revenue in three distinct ways. These numbers mean different things and cannot be added together.

Received
Cash-in-hand giving only: money that has actually arrived. One-time gifts and stock gifts that have been processed appear here.
⚠ One-time gifts and stock appear in BOTH Received and Committed—do not add the two categories together.
Committed
Expected giving only: pledges and commitments not yet fulfilled. Represents the full pledge amount regardless of how much has been paid.
⚠ Unlike in RE7, you cannot combine Received + Committed for a total—they overlap on one-time gifts and stock.
Recognition
Measures a donor’s overall value—typically lifetime giving or another recognition-based total. Used for donor acknowledgment levels and giving clubs.
Includes soft credits and may differ from cash totals.

Standard Reports

Standard Reports use the data entered into the database to generate formatted outputs for analysis, auditing, and sharing. Unlike dashboards (which are visual and interactive), reports produce structured documents with precise data that can be filtered, exported, or scheduled.

What you can do with reports

Track effectiveness
Measure how well your actions and solicitations are converting to gifts.
Identify giving patterns
See details about who is giving, when, how much, and to what campaigns.
Compare donors
Benchmark individual giving against cohort averages or prior-year performance.
Watch campaign progress
Monitor how a campaign, fund, or appeal is performing against goal in real time.
A query is not a report—but it can feed one A query selects records; a report formats and presents them. You can use a query to narrow the scope of a standard report—for example, using a constituent query to limit a Giving History report to a specific class year or geographic region.

Report output formats

Reports output in two formats only: PDF or CSV. Both must be downloaded—there is no preview in the browser. Large reports can take time to generate; for complex or data-heavy reports, use the scheduling feature to run them overnight.

Scheduling reports

TypeWhen to use
One-Time (scheduled)For large reports with significant data, schedule to run end-of-day or overnight so you don’t wait in the browser
RecurringFor reports you need on a regular schedule-weekly giving summary, monthly pipeline review-set once and receive automatically
Access and security Report tabs, parameters, and available report types vary based on your security level. Not all canned reports are available in the web view yet, some still require the database view. If you can’t find a report you expect to see, check with your database administrator.
Policies to establish for your team Before using reports for major decisions, your organization should have clear protocols for: how to handle anonymous gifts, whether to use pledge balance or full pledge amount, and how to include or exclude soft credits. Inconsistent practices across fundraisers create data discrepancies that undermine trust in your numbers.
